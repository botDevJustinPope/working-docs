## Codename rationale

*Proposed reading — codename predates this doc; user to confirm or correct.*

**Tactician** anchors on Rob's PO role. Three things from `data.md` point here together:

- **He owns the next move.** Per his own LinkedIn write-up, the PO mission is to *"craft a vision for the product that empowers our users and grows our business"* — backlog ownership, user-story authoring, sprint goal setting. The PO function *is* the wartime "what we are marking next": the operator who picks the objective.
- **He carries the longest product memory.** ~16 years on the same product family (OBIS → ePlan → BuildOn). The most-tenured persona on either of Atlas's two teams. A tactician's value is *pattern recognition over time* — Rob has seen what works and what doesn't across multiple eras of this product.
- **Cross-domain calibration.** Game programming → enterprise dev → founder → Scrum Master → PO. The career path matches a tactician's habit of reading the field from multiple angles before committing to a move.

The codename should read as *calls the move, never panics, has played this game before* — not as *armchair strategist*. Rob is on the field, not above it.

## WW2 unit identity

- **Rank / role analogue:** **Detachment Sergeant / Mission Planner** within the VDS Pathfinder Detachment. NCO-grade, not officer — runs the unit's day-to-day operational planning, briefs the team before each drop, signs off when the brief is final. Per the role table in `Teams/VDS/persona.md`: PO function maps directly onto Pathfinder doctrine — *"the unit moves only when the mission is set."*
- **Wartime function:** Receives the broader campaign direction from Atlas (the Company commander), translates it into specific drop assignments for the four-operator detachment, names the zone to mark, owns the brief that goes back to the follow-on force. When the team has two possible zones, Tactician calls. He does not crew the saw or run the wire — that is Ironforge's and Hawkeye's work — but he decides what *every operator works on next*.
- **Why this unit:** PO + Pathfinder NCO + 25-year product memory. Rob carries the institutional context that lets the detachment move quickly between drops without re-litigating fundamentals each time.

## Signature props / recurring artifacts

- **Leather-bound brief folio** — open in his hand or under one arm during planning scenes. Pages bear user-story fragments in his hand, with small wireframe sketches in the margin. The PO function rendered as a physical artifact.
- **Brass-tipped pointer-baton** — at his hip or in hand for war-room scenes. The "name the zone" gesture made literal. Era III treatment: the baton's tip carries a faint cyan emitter that projects the marker location into a holographic map.
- **Sergeant chevrons stenciled on the left pauldron** — small but consistent. NCO-grade, not officer; under Atlas, not equal to Atlas.

Avoid: a clipboard (too generic / too modern); a megaphone (wrong tone — Tactician is composed, not exhortative); a typewriter (his writing is on-the-go, not desk-bound).

- **Pose:** standing braced behind a wooden strategy table tilted into the frame, pointer-baton in one hand indicating a holographic / paper map, the other holding the brief folio open at his hip. Posture composed, never raised-voice.
- **Setting:** interior war room with a strategy table dominant in the foreground; in Era III, a rain-slick rooftop drop-zone war-room with holographic city-map projection above the table.
- **Mode:** **Mode B — Geometric propaganda poster** per `aesthetic.md` §5 default for single-figure portraits. Reads as composed authority.

## Voice / tone

- **Cadence:** measured. Opens with a one-line statement of where the team stands ("we have three drops queued"), then names the next move. Doesn't bury the lede.
- **Lexicon:** speaks in *drops*, *zones*, *markers*, *the next brief*, *the user-story we're holding*, *the wireframe that's not landing yet*. Two registers — strategic (vision, roadmap, theme) and operational (sprint, user story, acceptance criteria) — and he moves between them deliberately. References the *user* often; not the *stakeholder* by default.
- **Quirks:** references prior product cycles by year ("the way we handled this in '21..."). Comes back to *first principles* when a debate stalls. Uses "we" more than "I" — a Scrum-Master-tenure hangover.
- **Never:** writes a brief he hasn't pictured in his head first. Skips the wireframe step. Treats the team like a backlog-execution queue rather than the unit that names the next move with him.

## Tagline / motto

> **"PLAN THE DROP. NAME THE ZONE."**

Imperative duo per `aesthetic.md` §8. Encodes the two PO moves: *plan* (the brief, the user story, the sprint) and *name the zone* (pick the objective, commit the marker). Already in use as the Era III profile-card tagline.

Alternates considered:

- "EVERY DROP. EVERY BRIEF. EVERY MARKER." *(noun-phrase triplet, parallel to Indago's; clean but loses the imperative punch.)*
- "READ THE FIELD. CALL THE MOVE." *(more strategist-flavored; works for Era I, weaker for Era III.)*

Primary recommendation stays **"PLAN THE DROP. NAME THE ZONE."**.
