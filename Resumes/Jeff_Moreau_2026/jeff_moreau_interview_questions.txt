SECTION 2 — BACKEND PATTERNS: SQL-FIRST LEGACY → DDD WITH EF CORE

Context for us: a lot of our codebase is a SQL-first / data-centric legacy paradigm
(logic living in stored procedures and data-driven services), and we're working toward
a Domain-Driven Design model layered on EF Core. We want to hear how he thinks about
that transition and where his experience and knowledge actually sit.

These are "walk me through how you think" questions — give him room to explain.
(Phone screen: he nailed the EF change-tracking concept conceptually, but his EF/SQL
specifics are dated and he showed no architecture vocabulary. These questions gauge how
quickly the rust comes off and whether DDD is within reach for him.)

- A lot of legacy .NET is "SQL-first": the database and stored procedures are the
  source of truth, and the C# is a thin layer over them. How much of your work has
  looked like that, and what do you see as the real strengths and the pain points of
  that style?

- When you hear "Domain-Driven Design," what does that mean to you in practice? Walk me
  through the concepts you actually use — aggregates, entities vs value objects,
  repositories, bounded contexts, ubiquitous language — and where you've applied them.

- Where do you think business rules should live — in the database (stored procs,
  triggers, constraints), in a service layer, or in a rich domain model? Has that view
  shifted for you across your roles?

- Say we hand you a SQL-first system with fat stored procedures and an anemic C# layer,
  and the goal is to move toward a DDD model on EF Core without a full rewrite. How
  would you approach it? Where would you start, and how do you keep the app shippable
  along the way?

- How do you map a domain model onto EF Core when the relational schema already exists
  and can't change much — owned types, value-object mapping, keeping aggregate
  boundaries honest against the existing tables?

- What pulls you toward putting logic in the domain model versus leaning on the
  database? And when is "SQL-first" actually the right call — where would you leave it
  alone rather than DDD-ify it?
